    Chapter 1#

    • Sound beings with the vibration of an object
      • Sound transmitted by a medium
    • Music is part of the world of sound

    Pitch: Highness or Lowness of Sound#

    • Pitch → Highness or lowness of a sound
    • Sound that has a definite pitch is called a tone
      • Has a specific frequency
      • Vibrations of a tone are regular and reach the ear at regular intervals
      • Tones will sound different with different pitches
    • Interval of an octave - interval between first and last tones of the scale
      • Octave has seven different pitched before arriving at the high note which duplicates the first one
      • 7 tones on the keyboard
      • Added 12 tones are duplicated in higher and lower octaves
    • Distance is known as the pitch range
    • Most music is based on definite pitches

    Dynamics#

    • Dynamics → Degrees of loudness or softness are known as
      • Loudness or softness affects the type of the sound
    • Italian terms indicate dynamics
    • Ways to indicate dynamical changes
    • Not super precise
      • Tone has a dynamic level - in relation to other tones around the instrument or vocal

    Tone Color#

    • Tone color or Timbre descriptions
      • Bright
      • Dark
      • Brilliant
      • Mellow
      • Rich
    • Even though instruments might sound the same, the sound that the instrument creates will likely vary

    Chapter 2#

    Voices#

    • Singing demands a greater control of pitch and volume
    • Range of singers voice depends on training and physical makeup
    • Professionals usually have a vocal range of about 2 octaves

    Musical Instruments#

    • Any mechanism other than a voice that produce musical sounds

    • Most instruments have a wider range than voices

    • 6 categories of music

      • Woodwind
      • Brass
      • String
      • Percussion
      • Keyboard
      • Electronic

    • Instruments have different sizes for different sound ranges

    • Instruments are popular based off of the barrier to entry to the general public

    • Compositions can be written for more than one person and to large orchestras

      A diagram of an orchestra and the different sections of the orchestra

    String Instruments#

    • Instruments
      • Violin
      • Viola
      • Cello
      • Double Bass
      • All instruments are similar in setup and are made in the same manner
    • Strings have greatest versatility and expressive range
    • Pizzicato → Plucked string
    • Double Stop → Drawing the bow across two strings
      • Can also be with 3 or 4 strings
    • Vibrato → Throbbing expressive tone while vibrating the string using the fingerboard
    • Mute → Veil or muffle the tone
    • Tremolo → Very high-pitched tones

    Woodwind Instruments#

    • Produce vibrations of air within a tube
      • Traditionally made of wood
      • All have holes along the length of the instrument that are opened or closed by a key mechanism - similar to how the sound of the recorder changes when you cover the holes
    • Woodwinds are very different from each other in terms of tone color
    • Reed- piece of cane
    • Flute- blow over the edge of a mouth hole

    Brass Instruments#

    • Main instruments
      • Trumpet
      • French Horn
      • Trombone
      • Tuba
    • Mouthpiece - cup shaped funnel that you blow into to play
    • Tube is flared to form a bell at the end of the instruments
    • Mute alters the tone color - can be inserted into the bell
    • Very powerful- used for climaxes in pieces or for powerful. Also given solo passages

    Percussion#

    • Struck with hand, sticks or hammers or shaken or rubbed
    • Vibrations of percussion instruments are caused by stretched membranes for certain instruments or by plates, bars ,etc.
    • Percussion instruments have been used to emphasize rhythm, build suspense, etc.
    • Oft used by Jazz Musicians

    Keyboard instruments#

    • Instruments
      • Piano
    • Invented around 1700- perfected by the 1850’s
    • Sound produced by strings and hammers
    • The more that you hit the string, the louder it will be
    • Pedals to sustain the tone, veil tone, sustains some tones, not others
    • Harpsichord
      • 1775- replaced by pianos
      • Harpsichord
      • Organ
      • Accordion
      • Pipe Organ
    • Often used for religious ceremonies
      • Accordian
    • Most used in folk setting

    Electronic Instruments#

    • Produce or amplify sound via electronic means
      • Invented around 1904
      • Significant impact on music since 1950
    • Types
      • Electric piano
      • Electric Organ
      • Electric Guitars
      • Synthesizers
    • Generate, modify, and control sound
    • Can be played via a keyboard
    • 1960’s-70’s smaller, less expensive keyboards developed
    • Allow for the manipulation of sounds
    • MIDI, synthesizers work like a Piano
    • Tape Studio
      • Main tool in the 1950’s
      • Les Paul,etc
      • Including recorded sounds
      • Manipulated sounds
    • Sampling enables composers to hear what their music sounds like using digital technology
    • Digital Audio Workstations → DAWs

    Chapter 3#

    Rhythm#

    • Recurring pattern of tension and release
      • Refers to the ordered durations of sounds and silence

    Beat#

    • Regular, recurrent pulsation
    • Units of time in which all tones are measured
    • Combination of different durations of tones creates the rhythm

    Meter#

    • All of this is part of meter
    • Some beats feel stronger or more stressed than others
    • Meter → The organization of beats into regular groups
    • Measure or Bar → A group containing a fixed number of number of beats
    • Downbeat → The first or stressed beat of the measure
    • Quadruple meter → 4 beats to the measure, the downbeat is the strongest
      • Jazz and Rock are usually quadruple meter
    • Sextuple Meter→ 6 rather quick beats to the measure

    Accent and Syncopation#

    • Individual notes are stressed - how they get special emphasis
      • Accent - note is emphasized by being played louder than the notes around it
      • Syncopation → Accented note comes where there one is not expected
    • When an offbeat note is accented

    Tempo#

    • Tempo is the speed of the beat
      • Fast → associated of energy, drive and excitement
      • Slow → Solemn, lyrical and calm mood
    • Tempo indication is usually given at beginning of a piece in Italian
    • Accelerando - quickening of the tempo
    • Ritardando - becoming slower

    Chapter 4- Notating Pitch#

    • Horizontal lines on a staff are called ledger lines
    • Seven pitches/tones are: A, B, C, D, E, F, G
      • Repeated over to represent the same tones in higher and lower octaves
      • Other 5 tones of the octave correspond to the black keys of the piano
    • Indicated by sharp sign or a flat sign
    • Natural sign cancel a previous sharp or flat sign
      • Clef is placed at the beginning of the staff
    • Treble clef- used for high ranges
    • Bass clef- used for low ranges

    Notating Rhythm#

    • Nation does not indicate the exact duration of tones, it shows how long oen tone lasts in relation to others in the same piece
      • Depends on how the note is written- with stems or flags
    • To lengthen a notes, we can make it a dotted note - increases the duration of the note by a half
    • Tue also lengthens the duration of the note- connected by a tie
    • Rhythmic variety - triplet, etc.

    Notating Meter#

    • Time signature/ meter signature shows the meter of a peice

    • Appears at the beginning of the staff at the start of a piece

    Chapter 5#

    Melody#

    • Melody → Series of single tones that add up to add up to a recognizable whole
      • Has direction, shape and continuity
      • Conveys tension and release, expectation and arrival
      • Melodic curve or line
    • Climax - The highest note of a melody
    • Legato → Sung or played in a smooth or connected setup
    • Staccato → May be performed in a short or detached matter
    • Letters commonly used for discussing the segments of the peices
      • lowercase for shorter sections (a,b,etc)
      • Uppercase letters for longer sections (A,B,etc)

    Melody and Words#

    • Melody expresses the meaning and emotion of the words
    • Melody can evoke feeling in the listeners

    Chapter 6- Harmony#

    • Harmony refers to the way chords are constructed and how they follow each other

    Consonance and Dissonance#

    • Consonance → A tone combination that is stable
    • Dissonance → A tone combination that is unstable
      • Tension demands an onward motion to a stable
    • Resolution → Dissonance has its resolution when it moves to a consonance
    • Consonance and dissonance can exist in varying degrees
      • Some or more or less stable than other

    The Triad#

    • Triad → Most basic chord
    • Harmony- simultaneous tones in music that add support and richness to melody
    • Tonic chord → The main chord of the piece
    • Subdominant chord - the triad based on the fourth note of the scale
    • Dominant chord- built on the fifth note
    • Cadence - means both the resting point at the end of the melodic phrase

    Broken Chords (Arpeggios)#

    • Broken Chord or Arpeggio - Individual tones of a chord are sounded one after another

    Chapter 7- Key#

    • Key involves not only a central tone but also a central scale or chord
    • Scale is the basic pitches of a piece of music arranged from low to high

    The Major Key#

    • The major scale has two kinds of intervals:
      • Half-steps and whole-steps
    • Half-step is the smallest interval traditionally used in western music
    • Whole step is twice as large as the half step

    The Minor Scale#

    • Consists of seven different tones and an eighth tone that duplicates the first an octave higher
    • Minor scale can begin on any of the twelve tones in an octave
      • Thus, there are 12 minor scales

    Major and Minor keys#

    • When a piece is based off of a major scale → Major Key
    • When a piece of music is based off of a minor scale → Minor Key
    • To indicate the key of a music - composer uses a key signature

    The Chromatic Scale#

    • Twelve tones of the octave form the chromatic scale
      • Tones of the chromatic scale same distance apart, unlike major and minor scales
    • Tones are one half-step apart
    • Chromatic scale does not define a key
      • Contribute to a sense of motion and tension

    Modulation- Change of key#

    • Short pieces of music - single key from beginning to end
    • Longer pieces of music, variety or contrast created by using more than one key
    • Modulation → Shifting from one key to another within the piece

    Tonic Key#

    • No matter the amount that a piece has been changed, there is usually only one home note
    • Modulation is moving away from the home key, but then eventually returning to the home key

    Chapter 8- Musical Texture#

    • Musical texture - refers to how many different layers of sound are heard at a time
      • Described as:
    • Dense
    • Thin
    • Thick
    • Heavy
    • Light

    Monophonic Texture#

    • Monophonic → Having one sound
      • Singing alone
      • Single line with the same pitch in unison makes a stronger sound than singing alone

    Polyphonic Texture#

    • Polyphonic → Having many sounds
      • Each line of the piece enriches the other
    • Counterpoint → Combining several melodic lines into a whole
    • Round → song in which the same melody is sung or played at slightly different times

    Homophonic Texture#

    • Homophonic → One main melody accompanied by chords
    • Chords are subordinate to the melody

    Chapter 9- Musical forms#

    • Form → organization of musical elements in time

    Techniques that Create Musical Form#

    • Repetition → Creates a sense of unity
    • Contrast → Provides variety
    • Variation → keeps some elements of musical thought while changing others - gives a work unity and variety at the same time

    Repetition#

    • Appeals to the pleasure we get in recognizing and remembering something
    • Passage of time influences our reaction to repetition

    Contrast#

    • Forward motion, conflict and change of mood

    Variation#

    • Some ideas will be retained while others are changed
    • Melody might be restated with a different accompaniment

    Types of Musical form#

    ABA Form#

    • Contrast between A and B and then return to

    AB (Binary form)#

    • Composition made up of two section is in two part form
    • AABB

    Chapter 10- Musical Style#

    • Style - refers to a characteristic was of using melody, rhythm, tone color
      • The was these elements are combined can result in a total sound that’s unique
    • Musical styles can change from one era to the next
      • History of western art can be divided into the periods:
    • Middle Ages (450-1450)
    • Renaissance (1450-1600)
    • Baroque (1600-1750)
    • Classical (1750-1820)
    • Romantic (1820-1900)
    • Twentieth century to 1945
    • 1945 to the present
