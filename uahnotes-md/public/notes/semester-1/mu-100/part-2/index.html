    Chapter 11- Music in the Middle Ages#

    • Most important musicians were priests and worked for the church
      • Liturgical singing
      • Boys received music education in schools associated with churches and cathedrals
    • Most medieval was vocal- musicians also performed on a wide variety of instruments
      • “pronouncing the words of the Holy spirit”
    • Instruments incorporated into church music after 1800
    • We don’t know much about how medieval music sounded- most of the instruments have been destroyed

    Gregorian Chant#

    • Official music of the Roman Catholic church for over 1000 years
    • Designed to enhance specific parts of religious services
    • Conveys calm, otherworldly quality
      • Flexible rhythm, no meter, little sense of beat
      • Almost an improvisational character, due to the lack of beat and flexible
    • Named after Pope Gregory 1= 590-604
    • Originally passed down through oral tradition, later notated
    • Composers completely unknown

    The Church Modes#

    • Gregorian chant results partly from unfamiliar scales
      • “Church modes”
    • Same as major and minor scales, but half steps are different

    Secular music in the Middle Ages#

    • Secular music → Music outside of the church
      • 13th century
      • Dance in wooded places, parties, etc.

    Troubadour and Trouvere Song#

    • Active in courts of the nobility and in towns during the 12th and 13th centuries
      • Troubadours- Live in southern France and write poems in the Provencal language
      • Trouveres- Flourish in northern France and write in Old French
    • Mostly about courtly/idealized love for the idea noble lady
    • Different backgrounds- some nobles, some have modest backgrounds
    • Notated as single melodic lines
      • Monophonic texture

    Minstrels#

    • Entertained in castles, public places, etc.
      • Perform music, told stories
    • Lowest social level with no civil rights
    • Not many found steady work
    • Important source of info because there were no newspapers

    The Development of Polyphony: Organum#

    • Type of polyphony
    • Original music in this time had a single melodic line
    • Monks in monasteries begin to add a second melodic line to Gregorian chant
      • Originally improvised
    • Duplication of original melody at diff pitch
      • Developed a melodic curve of its own
    • 1100- not restricted to a note against not style
    • Lines could differ rhythmically as melodically

    School of Notre Dame: Measured Rhythm#

    • 1150- Paris becomes a center of polyphonic music
      • Measured rhythm - definite time values and clearly defined meter
      • Notation describes precise rhythm and pitches
      • By modern standards, medieval polyphony is hollow and thin
    • Lack of triads
      • 2 intervals of a third

    14th Century Music: The “New Art” in France#

    • ars nova
    • Secular music becomes more important than sacred music
    • Composer could specify any rhythmic pattern
      • Beats can be subdivision
      • Syncopation becomes important
    • Leonin and Perotin - measured rhythm, definite time values and rhythms
      • Notation indicated precise rhythms as well as pitches

    Guillaume de Machaut#

    • Born in French province of Champagne
    • Spent much of his life in the service of many royal families
    • Traveled to many courts and presented his music to various royals
    • Music consists mostly of love songs
    • Mass Ordinary -texts that remain the same from day to day throughout a church year

    Chapter 2: Music in the Renaissance#

    • 1450-1600
    • Renaissance idea of the “universal man”
    • Church remains an important patron of music but musical activity gradually shifts to the courts
      • Church choirs grow in size
      • People in positions of power compete for the finest composers and musicians

    Characteristics of Renaissance Music#

    Words and Music#

    • Vocal music more important than instrumental music
      • Humanistic interest in language vocal music
    • Close relationship between words and music
      • “Word painting” → Musical depiction of specific words
      • Emotion expressed in a moderate, balanced way, no extreme contrasts of dynamics

    Texture#

    • Renaissance music is chiefly polyphonic
      • Imitation among the voices is common
      • Homophonic texture with successions of chords is also used
    • Sounds much fuller than medieval music
    • Did not need instrumental accompaniment

    Rhythm and Melody#

    • Gentle flow than a defined beat
    • Each singer must maintain an individual rhythm

    Sacred Music in the Renaissance#

    • Motet and the mass are the two forms of Renaissance music
      • Motet
    • Polyphonic choral work set to a sacred Latin text
      • Mass
    • Polyphonic choral composition made up of five sections

    Josquin Desprez and the Renaissance market#

    • Compositions have masses, motes, and secular vocal pieces
    • Ave Maria
      • Opening uses polyphonic imitation
      • Short melodic phrase is presented by a soprano voice and then imitated in turn by the alto, tenor, and bass
      • Motet- 2,3 or 4 voices can be heard at the same time
    • Duets between high voices imitated by the lower parts
    • Almost homophonic at times

    Palestrina and the Renaissance Mass#

    • Palestrina music includes 104 and 450 sacred works
    • 1500s and Catholic church was challenged and questioned by the Protestants
    • Jesuit order founded to strengthen the church
    • Convening of the council of Tent considers questions of dogma and organization
      • Church music should be composed to inspire religious contemplation
    • Palestrina’s emphasis works reflect an emphasis on more spiritual music

    Pope Marcellus Mass#

    • Kyrie - rich polyphonic texture
      • Curved melody
      • Upward leaps balanced by downward steps

    Secular Music in the Renaissance#

    Vocal Music#

    • Secular music becomes increasingly popular
    • Development of music printing helps to increase the spread of music
      • Music becomes an important leisure activity
    • Renaissance secular music was written for groups of solo voices and for solo voice with the accompaniment of one or more instruments
    • Madrigal - a piece for several solo voices set to a short poem, usually about love
      • Originated during a creative explosion in Italy around 1520
      • Uses word painting and unusual harmonies more often

    Thomas Weelkes#

    • Written to Honor Queen Elizabeth

    Renaissance Lute Song#

    • Lute is a plucked string instrument
    • Lute song cultivated from the late 1590s to the 1620s
      • Mostly homophonic in texture

    John Dowland#

    • “Flow my Tears”
      • ABC section repeated - AA BB CC
      • Song about hopelessness but has uplifting moments

    Instrumental Music#

    • Becomes more important during the Renaissance
      • Able to be free from vocal music in the 16th century
      • Instrumental music intended for dancing
    • Renaissance musicians distinguished between loud and soft instruments

    Michael Praetorius#

    • Passamezzo and Galliard pair contrasting court dances and triple meter
