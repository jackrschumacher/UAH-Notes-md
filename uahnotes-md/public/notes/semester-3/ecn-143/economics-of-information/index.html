    Asymmetric Informaiton#

    • Asymmetric information
      • Information available to buyers and sellers differs
          In a market with asymmetric information, buyers and sellers have different information about the good being traded.
      In a market with asymmetric information a good is said to have hidden characteristics if the buyer or the seller observes something about the good that the other does not.
          In the car market, the seller of the car knows more about the car than the buyer
    • Hidden characteristics
      • One side observes something about the good being transacted that is both relevant and not observed by the other party
    • Hidden actions
      • When one side takes action that are relevant for but not observed by the other party
      • If the information gaps are large enough - possible for a market to completely shut down, even if there may be possible gains
    • Adverse selection
      • One agent in a transaction about a hidden characteristic of a good and decides whether to participate in the transaction on the basis of the information
      • Can be solved in the market by using signaling
            Example: high-risk and low-risk individuals
        Health insurance will attract a disproportionate number of high-risk individuals
        These are the individuals health insurance companies do not want to attract!  
        Therefore:
        Healthier people decide that it is not worth it to pay the extra price for the expensive healthcare plans- therefore the percentage of unhealthy people on the most expensive plans increases
            80 percent of the total number of people in Genovia with health insurance are more than 40 years old.
    • Signaling
      • An action that an individual with private information takes in order to convince others about his information

    Additional definitions#

    • Principal-agent relationship
      • Principal designs a contract specifying payments to the agent as a function of his or her performance, and the agent takes an action that influences performance and thus the payment of the principal amount
      • The principal provides incentives for another party to work on the principal’s benefit
      • Moral hazard
        • Actions that are taken by one party but are relevant for and not observed by the other party in the transaction
            Example: people with air bags in their cars are more likely to get into accidents.
        Why? Two explanations
        1. Adverse selection—if you know that you are a bad driver, you are more likely to choose a car that has air bags.
        2. Moral hazard—if you are protected from some of the risk of driving, you are more likely to drive recklessly.

    • Efficiency wages
      • Wages above the lowest pay that the workers would accept
      • Used by employers to boost productivity
