### English Society in the Fourteenth Century
      - **3 estates:** Clergy, Nobles (Lords, Vassals, Knights (gentles))
        - **Clergy**
          - Religious officials like priests, monks, nuns and bishops
          - Held significant spiritual and political influence, often advising rules and controlled a large amount of wealth
          - Enjoyed privileges- such as not paying taxes in some cases
        - **Nobles**
          - The landholding aristocracy, including kings, lords and knights
          - Commanded political power and military strength, granted land to nobles in exchange for loyalty and military service
          - Held a high social rank and controlled large landholdings
        - **Peasants**
          - Largest social group, consisting of farmers and laborers
          - Held the least power in the social hierarchy
          - Included everyone else:
            - Included serfs (bound to the land)
            - Freemen (who had more autonomy but still owed obligations to lords)
              - Bore burden of taxes and labor
      - **1347-49 Black Plague:** England's pre-plague population around 4 million
        - Possibly 50% population loss to plague
      - **1349 Ordinance of Laborers:** Fixed wages and required all under 60 years of age to work
        - Ordinance comes from the crown
        - Crown wishes to increase the labor force
      - **1351:**  Statute of Laborers set wages at pre-plague levels
### Language after the Plague
      - Primary language of the English nobility was French
      - Primary language of law was Latin
      - English spoken by all but most prominently the language of the commoner
      - 1362 Statute of Pleading \rarr Because of the death of so many legal clerks and otherwise, French became much less known, **act calls for English to be used primarily in the courts**.
        - Due to the plague
> The laws, customs, and statutes. . . are not commonly known . . . since they are pleaded, counted, and judged in the French language, which is very much unknown . . . , so that the people who plead or are impleaded in the king's courts and the courts of others have no understanding or knowledge of what is said for them or against them.
