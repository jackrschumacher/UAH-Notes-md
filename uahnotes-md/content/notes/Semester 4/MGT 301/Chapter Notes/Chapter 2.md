- ### Fundamentals of Entrepreneurship
- **Entrepreneurship**: Identification — Evaluation — Exploitation of **Entrepreneurial Opportunities**
- **Entrepreneurial Opportunity**: An occasion to create new products/services sold at a price greater than their cost of production
- ### The Entrepreneurship Process
- **Three Stages**:
	1. **Opportunity Identification**
	2. **Opportunity Evaluation**
	3. **Opportunity Exploitation**

- ### Types of Entrepreneurial Opportunities
  **Five Main Types**:  
- Creation of new products and services
- Discovery of new geographical markets
- Creation or discovery of new raw materials (or new uses for existing ones)
- Discovery of new methods of production
- New methods of organizing
  
  **Failure Rates** (Table 2.1):  
- New restaurants: ~51% fail within first 5 years
- New businesses: ~60% fail within first 6 years
- New chemical plants: ~80% fail within first 10 years
- ### Opportunity Identification
- Entrepreneurs must first know opportunities exist
- Not everyone identifies/exploits the same opportunities due to nonstandard opportunities and individual differences

- Five key factors (individual differences, prior knowledge, etc.)
- ### Opportunity Evaluation
- Determine if a good idea is a **viable opportunity**
- **Feasibility Analysis**: Study of the practicality of an idea to support a business
  
  **Key Biases Affecting Risk Perception**:  
- **Law of Small Numbers**: Relying on small samples of information to make decisions
- **Illusion of Control**: Overestimating personal control over outcomes
- ### Opportunity Exploitation
- Activities and investments to gain returns from new products/services
- Exploit when:
	- Customers will value the new offering **and** there is market demand
	- You have support of important stakeholders
	- Management team is perceived as capable
- ### Financing the Exploitation Stage
  **Internal Financing**:  
- Entrepreneur’s own funds or credit cards
  
  **External Financing** (3 main forms):  
	1. **Angel Investors**: Wealthy individuals who provide capital (smaller investments, often in areas of expertise, take equity)
	2. **Venture Capitalists**: Investment firms that supply larger funds (take equity, focus on high-growth potential)
	3. **Bank Financing**: Loans from financial institutions (focus on repayment ability, not long-term potential)

- ### Corporate Entrepreneurship
- Process where an individual or group creates a **new organization within an existing corporation**
- Works in both new and established firms; new units stay closely tied to the parent company
  
  **Four General Types**:  
- Sustained Regeneration
- Organizational Rejuvenation
- Strategic Renewal
- Domain Definition
- ### Social Entrepreneurship
- **Commercial Entrepreneurship**: Focus on generating sales and profits for personal/shareholder wealth
- **Social Entrepreneurship**: Recognition, evaluation, and exploitation of opportunities that create **social value** (vs. personal wealth)
  
  **Key Differences**:  
- **Mission**: Profits vs. public value
- **Resources**: External investors vs. donations
- **Performance Measurement**: Quantitative (profit) vs. social impact
  
  **Success Factors in Social Entrepreneurship**:  
- Strong networks of relationships (capital, employees, volunteers)
- Access to capital (mostly donations, harder than commercial)
- Public acceptance of the new venture (depends on alignment with social values)
